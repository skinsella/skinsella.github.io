Gerard at [Turbulence Ahead](http://www.turbulenceahead.com/2008/09/education-20.html) posts a nice piece of news that a school in Dublin is to give it's students ebook readers. I'm all for this sort of thing, mainly because I'm a nerd, but also because I think technologies like this help students learn.

But why just an ebook reader? Why not give every kid in the class a[netbook](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netbook) and see what happens?

Think [OLPC](http://www.laptop.org/), but for the children of the developed countries.

I'm currently reading [Born Digital](http://www.borndigitalbook.com/), a book all about our children as the first generation born into a fully digital culture, and what that means. It makes for great reading, and throws our [education system](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education) into relief as the 19th Century construction it really is.

Also, [this teacher is now using Google Docs](http://googledocs.blogspot.com/2008/08/tips-for-introducing-online.html) to help his students learn how to collaborate on projects. The students are 10.

It is instructive that when I was 10, the [Commodore 64](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodore_64)was the best computer the average middle class kid could get his hands on. [Now? a 1.6ghz, 1gig ram, 80gb hard drive, 1lb machine](http://www.google.ie/url?sa=t&source=web&ct=res&cd=2&url=http%3A%2F%2Fen.wikipedia.org%2Fwiki%2FAspire_One&ei=sXu_SIKhFJSgwwHezPDiDw&usg=AFQjCNFUmOswwbtIgiAeSYVgtRza5tOw9w&sig2=PMtMwi5W4gQCGWv9uKTijg) can be bought in [Dunnes Stores](http://www.dunnesstores.com) for 299 euros, less than the cost of a games console.

We live in the future, we really do.
