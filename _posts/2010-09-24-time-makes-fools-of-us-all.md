Morgan Kelly has a chapter in [the book I've edited](http://www.amazon.com/Understanding-Irelands-Economic-Crisis-Prospects/dp/184218198X%3FSubscriptionId%3D0PZ7TM66EXQCXFVTMTR2%26tag%3Dstephkinse-20%26linkCode%3Dxm2%26camp%3D2025%26creative%3D165953%26creativeASIN%3D184218198X). Getting ready for the promotional part of the book, I went looking for something a few minutes ago, and found a succession of my own posts on this blog referencing Professor Kelly. Reading through the posts, the turn is fascinating. I go from thinking, more than a little sarcastically, '[wow, this guy is a bit out there](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2008/08/15/prof-kelly-on-the-house-price-decline/), perhaps another [Roubini'](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2009/01/06/responding-to-the-crisis/) to [wholly](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2009/01/20/quote-of-the-day/) agreeing with him. [This guy got it right](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2010/09/09/but-no-one-saw-it-coming/). I have the image of the patient bleeding from two wounds in my mind when I speak about fiscal policy and banking policy, and most of the time, it's Kelly's assessment I'm [rehashing](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2010/09/01/spot-the-myths/), I suppose, in one form or another.

So: three cheers for Morgan Kelly. Time really does make fools of us all. Well, me at least.
