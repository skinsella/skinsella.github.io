[Lisbon 2008](http://www.lisbontreaty2008.ie/): What was it, was did it mean, and why did it fail?    
This lecture traces the history of the [EU's](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union) development through its treaty structure from the [Treaty of Rome](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaties_of_Rome) to the Treaty of Nice, and spends a large portion of the lecture focusing on the [Treaty of Lisbon](http://europa.eu/lisbon_treaty/index_en.htm), which the Irish electorate sought fit to reject on June 12, 2008.
This lecture will place Lisbon in context, and ask: where do we go from here? We'll wrap up with some introductory notes on trade and trade creation. 

Click below for[lecture notes](http://stephenkinsella.net/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2008/09/ec4333_lecture4_handout_numbers.pdf), [slides](http://www.slideshare.net/kinsells/ec4333-lecture-2-2008-lisbon-and-budgets-presentation/), [handouts](http://stephenkinsella.net/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2008/09/ec4333_lecture4_handout_numbers.pdf), a [podcast of the lecture](http://vimeo.com/1745703), and links. 

The EU is made up of a tissue of treaties which grant it supranational powers, for example, the EU acts as a customs union, and enforces environmental standards above the level of the nation state. The EU cannot tax and spend, however, and receives its income from donations from its member state of 1.27% of their [GDP](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product). 
The European coal and steel federation came into existence in 1951 following the [Treaty of Paris](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_%281951%29). 
The first treaty (actually two treaties, more on that in the lecture) to be ratified by [Belgium](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium), [France](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France), [Italy](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italy), [Luxembourg](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg), the [Netherlands](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netherlands) and [West Germany](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany), was the Treaty of Rome. Rome established the EEC and [EURATOM](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Atomic_Energy_Community), an atomic power clearing house. From there up to the present day, the EU has expanded its set of competencies, and Lisbon is only the latest in this line.
