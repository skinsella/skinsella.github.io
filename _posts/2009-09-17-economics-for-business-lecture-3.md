The concept of utility has a long and convoluted history in economics, and [has been discussed since at least 1748](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremy_Bentham). The idea of choice theory is to make a statement about what choices a [rational](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_choice_theory) person should make, based on their [preferences](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preference), among competing alternatives. It will turn out that people's desires are infinite, while their budgets are often much more limited. The constraints individuals work under, combined with their prestated preferences, turn out to be the important factors in creating the theory of choice. First, let's talk about utility.

**Important Concepts.** Budget constraint, [utility function](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utility), [indifference curve](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indifference_curve), marginal utility, [marginal rate of substitution](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_rate_of_substitution).

**Definition.** *Utility is the satisfaction a person receives from their economic activities.*

**Indifference Curves**

These are tricky little beasts, so it is wise to spend some time working on drawing them.

Think of an indifference curve like a [contour map](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_line) of a mountain. The indifference curve represents a set of points (called a locus) where, for each consumer, each point represents a combination of goods which makes them equally happy. The consumer will always want to be higher up the mountain of happiness, so to speak, but if they have to stay there, then they will be indifferent between the alternatives on that line.
