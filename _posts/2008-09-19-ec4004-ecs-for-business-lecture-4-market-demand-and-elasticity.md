Market demands will be represented by capital letters from now on, and individual demands by lower case letters. 

This lecture introduces the concept of elasticity. 

**Definition. ***Elasticity is the percentage change in one variable brought about by a one percent change in some other variable. *

The [price elasticity of demand](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_elasticity_of_demand),  

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_fa3ab9e0224fd3a1258939b05bc83408.gif)

, is defined mathematically as the logarithmic derivative of the market demand curve. But we don't need to be so technical. We can just define it in a point-wise way as the percentage [change in quantity demanded](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demand_curve) for a given **percentage** change in price, or

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_df0721a92bc6e92b5b87aa97cc094564.gif)

 (percentage change)

The price elasticity of demand looks at movements along a demand curve, and tells you him much in percentage terms quantity changes for each per cent change in price. 

**History. **Elasticity as a concept is borrowed from materials science and [applied mathematics](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics). The theory was developed to understand how metals stress under extremes of heat and cold. It was this theory which allowed [the Eiffel tower](http://maps.google.com/maps?ll=36.1125,-115.172222222&spn=0.01,0.01&q=36.1125,-115.172222222 (Eiffel%20Tower)&t=h) to be built. 

**Example**. We'll work out a numerical example in class, and I'll show you [a mathematica demonstration](http://demonstrations.wolfram.com/RevenueAndElasticity/) of elasticity at work on total revenue in a market. 

**Application. **We'll look at the effects of rising oil prices on driving patterns in the USA.
