**Introduction**

In the last few lectures, ([1](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2008/03/11/ec4004-lecture-1-growth-matters/), [2](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2008/03/12/ec4004-lecture-2-when-macreconomics-doesnt-work/), and [3](http://www.stephenkinsella.net/2008/03/26/ec4004-lecture-3-podcast/)) we've looked at growth theory and the stakes involved in getting the basics wrong. We saw the [Solow model's predictions](http://www.econlib.org/library/Enc/EconomicGrowth.html) about sustained capital accumulation (

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_a083d39c5297fe420aae7842df4c5e6e.gif)

): keep population growth low, keep savings (

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_03c7c0ace395d80182db07ae2c30f034.gif)

) and therefore investment(

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_dd7536794b63bf90eccfd37f9b147d7f.gif)

) rather high, and try to curb depreciation on assets. Technical progress and [human capital](http://www.econlib.org/library/Enc/HumanCapital.html) move the economy forward, potentially stimulating convergence of growth rates.

Now we'll move onto a micro-founded macro model. The basic idea is to specify four markets inside the economy: the products market, the bond market, the money market, and the labour market. We'll build our macroeconomic equilibria from behavioural assumptions about the actors in the model: households and firms. Households are assumed to want to maximise their incomes from all of these markets, subject to a budget constraint. Firms want to maximise profits. Their interactions, along with the usual macroeconomic accounting identities, such as 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_4f68f23dc5d7b14dcdc2d1c3dd7e9d25.gif)

, give us the macroeconomic equilibrium, called a general equilibrium.
