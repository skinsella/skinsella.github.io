The business cycle is a tricky concept. Economists have studied why economies experience large shifts in demand for good and services for centuries. [Many indicators of economic performance (or lack thereof) exist](http://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~nroubini/bci/bci.html), and as many theories exist to explain them.

You'll also want to read [Barro](http://www.amazon.co.uk/gp/redirect.html%3FASIN=0324545673%26tag=adriaantijsse-20%26lcode=xm2%26cID=2025%26ccmID=165953%26location=/o/ASIN/0324545673%253FSubscriptionId=0PZ7TM66EXQCXFVTMTR2), chapter 8, before this lecture.

Economists pretend we can measure the business cycle, and make serious and deep efforts to understand it as a profession, but economists are largely powerless to do anything* about* [business cycles](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_cycle). We experience them like anyone else. Even the wikipedia article defining it is continually in dispute.

**What is a business cycle?**

Well, we call it a 'cycle' because there seems to be an up and down relationship between real (or actual) [GDP](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product) and trend (or potential) GDP. When an economy is below it's trend GDP, it might be heading into a recession. When an economy is above it's trend GDP in terms of it's real GDP, it might be experiencing a boom period.

GDP is assumed to break down into two parts, Real trend and cyclical. The cyclical part of GDP is the real part minus the trend part, and that is what we want to explain in this section of the course.

Barro shows us the proportionate deviation from trend GDP of the [US Economy](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_the_United_States). What does it look like for Ireland?
