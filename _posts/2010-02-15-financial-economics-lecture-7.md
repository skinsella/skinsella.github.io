We're going to introduce the [Capital Asset Pricing Model](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_asset_pricing_model), a toy model designed in the 1950s and 1960s with zero practical relevance for today's markets or tomorrow's market decision-makers (that's you). However, it's important you are able to talk the talk with other [finance](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance) professionals in the real world, to be able to understand when someone says "stock X has a [beta](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_%28finance%29) of 0.6", or somesuch. Hence today's lecture on optimal [portfolio](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portfolio_%28finance%29) construction and the model itself. [Here's a recording of the lecture also](http://www.vimeo.com/9579185).

So, building directly on our work in simple [probability](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability) and [random walks](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_walk) in finance, we'll have a look at the CAPM. Cool readings are below, the notes I gave out in class are [here](http://stephenkinsella.net/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2008/05/ec4024-lecture10-capm.pdf), the slides are h[ere](http://stephenkinsella.net/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2010/02/EC4024_2010_Lecture7.pdf), the[excel sheet I used is here](http://stephenkinsella.net/WordPress/wp-content/uploads/2008/05/ten-stocks-capm.xls), the mathematica demonstration is [here](http://demonstrations.wolfram.com/TwoAssetMarkowitzFeasibleSet/).
