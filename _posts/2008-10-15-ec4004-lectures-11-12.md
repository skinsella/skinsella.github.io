**1. Costs, Redux.**

Last week we looked at the theory of costs. We defined total cost, [marginal cost](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_cost), and [average cost](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_cost).

We saw that total cost was the sum of fixed costs and variables,

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_2b69c445e60f7fd6bf94fa134e810f39.gif)

, average cost was the division of total cost by the quantity of output produced, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_f527028c3d6a62bf39ff1106edf672d2.gif)

 and Marginal cost is the change in cost for a one unit change in output, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_bf88dbb51d18f83bd5f819c259ee63f3.gif)

. We saw their relationship using a [Mathematica demonstration](http://demonstrations.wolfram.com/ShortRunCostCurves/).

We saw the difference between short run costs and long costs. The short run has the disadvantage of fixed costs, which are costs associated with inputs one can't change in the short run. The long run allows one to be more flexible with respect to costs. When we look at per-unit short run [cost curves](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_curve), we see the  familiar relationship, as in the picture below. Play with these curves using the [Mathematica demonstration](http://demonstrations.wolfram.com/ShortRunCostCurves/).

**2. Profit Maximisation and Supply**

Firms exist to capture economies of scale by reducing transactions costs. This is [the Coase Theorem](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coase_theorem). The firm is assumed to want the most for itself and its shareholders or owners. The firm is assumed in simple models to try to maximize its profits, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_4f08e3dba63dc6d40b22952c7a9dac6d.gif)

, which we defined in the last lecture as

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_1b26bd72aa6ebc25445c5f24797d591c.gif)

,

where 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_0cff366295d0430b0d714ec4e628ec74.gif)

 represents the total revenues the firm gets for producing some level of output 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7694f4a66316e53c8cdd9d9954bd611d.gif)

, and 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_40d72fa1b7695f01ca0f0392dcdb43bb.gif)

 represents the total cost the firm incurs for producing some level of output, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7694f4a66316e53c8cdd9d9954bd611d.gif)

.

The firm has to choose what level of output 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7694f4a66316e53c8cdd9d9954bd611d.gif)

 to produce. Remembering the definition of marginal cost: 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_443dcde120dabfde7393e769090fbbd9.gif)

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c40441ae642a6ff0732851c2a89157fb.gif)

Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost.

Or:

MR = MC.

(It's really important to remember that one, so I'm putting it in red.)

How does the firm actually do this? By trial and error. The firm will experience 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_b69fe64643b4628b2aa37cd727f06a61.gif)

 for output levels below 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c40441ae642a6ff0732851c2a89157fb.gif)

, which tell it to produce more output, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7694f4a66316e53c8cdd9d9954bd611d.gif)

, until it equals or exceeds 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c40441ae642a6ff0732851c2a89157fb.gif)

. If the firm produces more output than 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c40441ae642a6ff0732851c2a89157fb.gif)

, it will experience 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_c65256917a0aac3c3053cb0cd26c4d25.gif)

, so it is losing money on each additional sale. There are a [fair few examples](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factory_Records) of this situation occurring in real life, notably in the music industry.

For example, a popular story about the New Order song, "Blue Monday" holds that the single's [die-cut](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Die_%28manufacturing%29) sleeve, created by Factory designer [Peter Saville](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Saville), cost so much to produce that [Factory Records](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factory_Records) actually lost money on each copy sold.

One important idea we'll have a look at in the lecture is the concept of the firm as **Price Taker. **

A** price taker** is a firm or individual whose decisions regarding buying or selling have no effect on the prevailing market price of a good or service. For a price taking firm it must hold that 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_cded0e2712b0752762fc7f994d41fa6b.gif)

If the firm experiences a downward-sloping [demand](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supply_and_demand) curve, then we can say with confidence that 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_4ea3186ae61d1460d02da1fef2415d8a.gif)

, because when you're selling one more unit, and that sale causes the price to decline, then you can be sure the firm's 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_d5c44258d51659f96279c470ce8185dc.gif)

 will be less than 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_44c29edb103a2872f519ad0c9a0fdaaa.gif)

. We'll show a [Mathematica demonstration](http://demonstrations.wolfram.com/ShortRunCostCurves/) of this property in class, if the program actually works for me this time 🙂

We'll also see how the [marginal revenue](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_revenue) of the price taking firm is dependent on the [price elasticity of demand](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_elasticity_of_demand) for that product. Recall that elasticity, 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_e1671797c52e15f763380b45e841ec32.gif)

, defined at a certain price 

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_83878c91171338902e0fe0fb97a8c47a.gif)

![](https://www.stephenkinsella.net/wp-content/plugins/latex/cache/tex_7694f4a66316e53c8cdd9d9954bd611d.gif)

, is defined as the percentage [change in quantity demanded](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demand_curve) divided by the percentage change in price.

(elasticity of demand)
